# How to Start a Now We See You Chapter at Your School

This guide is for students, teachers, or community members who want to bring *Now We See You* to a new school. Everything here is based on how the first chapter was built at Lake Washington High School, adapted into a repeatable checklist you can follow in order.

> **What you need before you start:** one or two committed student leads, a supportive staff advisor (a teacher, counselor, or administrator), and a willingness to spend more time listening than publishing. The project is built on consent, not speed.

---

## 1. Build your founding team

Start with 2–4 people. You need at least one person who can:

- **Lead interviews and write stories** — someone patient and comfortable talking to adults across different roles and backgrounds.
- **Create portraits or art** — hand-drawn portraits are part of the identity of the project, but photography or collaborative art can work while you find an artist.
- **Manage the website and QR codes** — someone who enjoys basic tech setup and can keep pages updated.
- **Coordinate with school staff** — a liaison who can navigate schedules, permissions, and admin communication.

You do not need every skill on day one. The founding team at Lake Washington began with one artist and one interviewer and grew from there.

---

## 2. Secure a staff advisor and school approval

Before you feature anyone, get formal support from your school. Schedule a short meeting with a principal, activities coordinator, or communications lead and explain:

- The project is a long-term, consent-based archive, not a social media campaign.
- Every featured staff member chooses exactly what is shared and can withdraw at any time.
- Content is hosted on the school-affiliated site, not personal accounts.
- You need a staff advisor to help with scheduling, access, and policy questions.

Ask for written approval or an email you can keep on record. This protects you and the staff you feature.

---

## 3. Define your first featured roles

Pick a small group of roles to start with. The first chapter focused on classified staff — the people who keep the building running but are rarely named in school publications. Good candidates include:

- Custodians and maintenance staff
- Food service workers
- Bookkeepers and front-office staff
- Paraeducators and instructional assistants
- Bus drivers
- Library technicians

Start with **one to three people** for your pilot. A small launch lets you test the process before scaling.

---

## 4. Nominate and ask for consent

Create a simple nomination form (paper or digital). Ask nominators: *Who is this person? What do they do? Why should they be featured?*

Then contact the nominee directly — in person, if possible. Explain the project and ask for consent in three parts:

1. **Consent to be interviewed and photographed or drawn.**
2. **Consent to publish specific quotes, photos, and a written bio.**
3. **Consent to keep a public profile and appreciation wall live on the site.**

Let them say no. Let them take time to decide. Let them review the final draft before anything goes live. Record their consent clearly and keep it secure.

---

## 5. Collect the story

Schedule a 20–40 minute interview in a quiet place. Good questions to start with:

- What is your official job title, and what does a typical day look like?
- How long have you worked here, and what did you do before?
- What is something most students or staff do not know about your work?
- What makes you feel seen or appreciated at school?
- Is there anything you want the school community to understand about your role?

Record audio only with permission. Take notes. Transcribe the parts that matter most. Then write a short bio and a longer story in the person’s own voice as much as possible. Share the draft with the nominee and ask for corrections.

---

## 6. Create the portrait and visual identity

Each profile should include a clear portrait. Hand-drawn or painted portraits are the signature of the original chapter, but the format is less important than the respect behind it. If you use photography, make sure the person is comfortable and the setting reflects their dignity — not just their uniform or their workplace.

At the same time, decide on your chapter’s visual style:

- A name (e.g., "Now We See You — [Your School Name] Chapter")
- A simple color palette and typography
- A consistent page layout for every profile

Keep it simple. The story should be the star.

---

## 7. Set up the website and profiles

You will need a small website to host profiles, a nomination form, and an appreciation wall. The basic structure is:

- **Home page** — mission, how it works, and links to featured profiles.
- **Gallery** — all published profiles with portraits and short bios.
- **Individual profile page** — portrait, full story, role, and a moderated appreciation wall.
- **Nominate page** — a form for community members to suggest the next featured staff member.
- **About page** — your founding team, school context, and contact information.

If you are technical, you can use a React/Vite/Tailwind setup similar to the original project. If you are not, tools like Carrd, Notion, Squarespace, or Google Sites can work for a first version. The most important part is publishing with consent, not the tool you use.

---

## 8. Create QR codes and link them to profiles

Print a QR code for each featured profile. Place it near the person’s workspace — a custodial closet, the front office, the cafeteria line, or the bus stop. When someone scans the code, they land on that person’s profile page.

Make sure the QR destination is a link you can update later. If a person moves to a new role or the URL structure changes, you should be able to redirect the QR code without reprinting it.

---

## 9. Launch with a small, respectful event

A launch does not need to be loud. Some ideas that worked well:

- A short hallway display with the portrait, a short quote, and the QR code.
- A morning announcement introducing the person and inviting people to scan the code.
- A handwritten thank-you board near the display where students can leave notes before they are moderated online.

Make sure the featured person is invited to the launch and can approve the final display before it goes up.

---

## 10. Moderate the appreciation wall

Public appreciation is powerful, but it needs moderation. Decide on clear rules and enforce them consistently. A good starting policy:

- Keep messages kind, specific, and school-appropriate.
- Do not allow jokes, gossip, romantic comments, or personal contact information.
- Review messages before they are published, or use AI moderation with human review as a backup.
- Give the featured person the right to remove any message from their wall.

---

## 11. Keep the chapter alive

After the first launch, the work is maintenance. Plan to:

- Feature one new person every 4–8 weeks.
- Keep a shared calendar of interview, review, and publish dates.
- Store consent forms and final drafts in one organized place.
- Train new team members each year so the chapter survives graduation.

Track what works. Count scans, note which displays get attention, and ask featured staff what they thought of the process. Use that feedback to improve the next cycle.

---

## The one rule that matters most

> **Nothing is published without the person’s clear, informed, revocable consent.**

That single rule protects the dignity of the people you are celebrating, and it is the reason the project lasts beyond a single campaign.

If you are ready to start, your first step is simple: pick one person, ask permission to talk, and listen.
